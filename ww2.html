Recruitment Posters

Uncle Sam

Description: The most popular Uncle Sam poster from World War II was created by James Montgomery Flagg, and featured Uncle Sam pointing his finger with the words "I Want You for U.S. Army."

Significance: While the image of Uncle Sam was created during World War I, its became widely popular as the representative image of the World War II propaganda. The image of Uncle Sam pointing his finger directly at the viewer with a serious expression serves as a powerful symbol of authority and urgency, aiming to mobilize civilians to join the military or contribute to the war effort in other ways. This poster, like many others from the era, utilizes the familiar figure of Uncle Sam to appeal to a broad audience, leveraging the symbol's historical significance and the trust it commands. The psychological impact of this poster lies in its ability to evoke a sense of duty and responsibility among the population, reinforcing the idea that every citizen has a role to play in the war effort.

Submarine Service

Description: The poster "He Volunteered for Submarine Service" features a man in a submarine uniform, with a woman looking at him with admiration.

Significance: This poster is significant for its psychological appeal, specifically targeting male instincts and societal norms of the time. It cleverly uses the image of a woman being attracted to a man in uniform, suggesting that volunteering for submarine service could make a man more appealing to women. This tactic is a reflection of the broader World War II propaganda efforts that sought to manipulate public sentiment and encourage participation in the war effort. By appealing to male instincts and societal expectations, the poster aims to motivate men to volunteer for the submarine service, which was considered one of the most dangerous and challenging roles during the war.

O'er the Rampants We Watch

Description: The poster "O'er the ramparts we watch: United States Army Air Forces" features a larger-than-life aviator holding a small bomb, towering amongst the clouds over a group of U.S. bomber aircraft.

Significance: The image of the aviator holding a bomb, towering amongst the clouds over a group of U.S. bomber aircraft, serves as a powerful symbol of authority and urgency, aiming to mobilize civilians to join the military or contribute to the war effort in other ways. This poster, like many others from the era, utilizes the familiar figure of the aviator to appeal to a broad audience, leveraging the symbol's historical significance and the trust it commands. The psychological impact of this poster lies in its ability to evoke a sense of duty and responsibility among the population, reinforcing the idea that every citizen has a role to play in the war effort.

Man the Guns

Description: The poster "Man the Guns, Join the Navy!" features a muscular male figure, emphasizing strength and readiness to fight.

Significance: This poster is significant for its psychological appeal, specifically targeting the sense of masculinity and pride in joining the war effort. The muscular male figure invokes a sense of masculinity and pride, suggesting that joining the navy is a noble and honorable endeavor. This tactic is a reflection of the broader World War II propaganda efforts that sought to manipulate public sentiment and encourage participation in the war effort.

When? Its up to you

Description: Description: The poster "When? It's up to you!" features airplanes flying over a caricature of Hitler, with a question mark and the text "When? It's up to you!" prominently displayed.

Significance: This poster is a powerful piece of World War II propaganda that effectively communicates a sense of urgency and personal responsibility. By posing the question "When?" and leaving the answer to the individual, it encourages viewers to reflect on their own actions and decisions in the context of the war. This poster is significant for its psychological impact, as it directly addresses the viewer, making them the subject of their own decision-making process. It instills a sense of urgency by implying that the time to act is now, and it emphasizes personal responsibility by suggesting that each individual has the power to influence the course of the war by enlisting.

Service Above Self

Description: The poster "Service Above Self!" from the Medical Department is a World War II propaganda poster that emphasizes the importance of service and selflessness in the war effort.

Significance: This poster is significant for its message of inclusivity and the value of service in the war effort. It highlights that even those who cannot serve in combat roles, such as medics, play a crucial part in the war. By focusing on the medical department, the poster aims to show that every individual, regardless of their physical capabilities, has a place in the war and can contribute significantly to the war effort. The emphasis on "Service Above Self" suggests that the act of serving, even in non-combat roles, is a form of selflessness and dedication to the cause.

War Bonds and Financing

Buy War Bonds

Description: The "Buy War Bonds Replica WWII Propaganda Poster" features a stirring image of Uncle Sam standing in the clouds, holding a billowing U.S. Flag.

Significance: This poster is one of several from the World War II era designed to encourage patriotism and support of the war effort through the purchase of war bonds. War bonds were crucial for controlling inflation and financing military operations during the war. Despite the hardships faced by the U.S. people, including rationing, the poster aimed to inspire them to step up and purchase these bonds, effectively loaning the government money and supporting the war effort. By featuring Uncle Sam, a symbol of the United States, it appeals to a sense of national pride and duty. The imagery of Uncle Sam standing in the clouds, holding the U.S. Flag, serves as a powerful visual representation of the country's commitment to the war and the need for financial support.

4 Favorites

Description: This poster features a superhero, likely inspired by characters from comic books, battling against the dictators of the Axis powers. The superhero is depicted as a powerful figure, using his strength and agility to defeat the enemies of the United States.

Significance: This poster is significant for its innovative use of popular culture to stimulate public support for the war effort. By incorporating the superhero genre, which was highly popular among children and families during the 1940s, the poster aimed to create a sense of excitement and engagement. The superhero, as a symbol of strength and courage, was used to represent the idea that by purchasing war bonds, individuals were contributing to the fight against the Axis powers, much like the superhero battling the villains. This approach was effective in making the war bonds seem like a fun and adventurous way to support the war effort, rather than a mundane financial invetsment.

Keep Us Flying

Description: This poster features a detailed portrait of a Tuskegee Airman, Robert W. Deiz, a notable figure in the history of the American Air Force.

Significance: The significance of this poster lies in its ability to connect the viewer to the war effort on a personal level. By featuring a Tuskegee Airman, it highlights the contributions of African American soldiers to the war effort, emphasizing their bravery and the importance of their role in the fight against the Axis powers. The message "Buy War Bonds" is not just a financial appeal but a call to action that directly supports the war effort. The poster suggests that by purchasing war bonds, individuals are contributing to the financial resources needed to keep the airmen, like Deiz, flying and fighting for the USA.

National Unity and Patriotism

United We Win

Description: The "United We Win" poster features a photograph by Alexander Liberman, depicting racial solidarity among wartime workers, with the slogan "United We Win" prominently displayed.

Significance: This poster uniquely addresses the issue of racial prejudice and segregation, which were prevalent in the U.S. military and society at the time. Despite these challenges, African Americans participated wholeheartedly in the fight against the Axis powers, often with the goal of ending racial discrimination in American society. The government was aware of the demoralizing effects of racial prejudice on the American population and its impact on the war effort, leading to the promotion of posters like "United We Win" to highlight the participation and achievement of African Americans in military and civilian life. The "United We Win" poster serves as a visual representation of the government's efforts to foster unity and patriotism among the American people.

Avenge Pearl Harbor

Description: The "Avenge Pearl Harbor. Our bullets will do it" poster features imagery and text that directly reference the attack on Pearl Harbor, using the emotional trauma of the event as a central theme to inspire action and unity among the American people.

Significance: This poster effectively leverages the emotional impact of the attack on Pearl Harbor to motivate the American public to join the war effort. By framing the war effort as a means to avenge the attack on Pearl Harbor, the poster taps into the deep-seated emotions of anger, resentment, and a desire for justice that were prevalent among the American people following the attack. The significance of this poster lies in its ability to harness the collective anger and desire for retribution following the Pearl Harbor attack to mobilize the American public. By focusing on the attack as the primary motivation for the United States to enter the war, the poster effectively promotes unity and patriotism among the American people.

Remember Pearl Harbor

Description: The "Remember Pearl Harbor" music disc by Swing and Sway with Sammy Kaye

Significance: This music disc played a significant role in the cultural and emotional landscape of the United States during World War II. By releasing a song that directly references the attack on Pearl Harbor, the record aimed to inspire unity and patriotism among the American people. The use of Big Band music, which was popular during the war, further emphasized the collective effort and the importance of community in overcoming the challenges of the war. The record's release coincided with the height of the war effort, making it a timely piece that resonated with the public's desire for retribution and unity.

Help Win the War, Squeeze in One More

Description: The "Help win the war. Squeeze in one more" poster, created by Lee Morehouse, is a World War II propaganda piece that encourages the American public to contribute to the war effort by participating in car pools.

Significance: This poster is significant for its emphasis on the power of small, individual actions in contributing to the war effort. By focusing on the practical and immediate action of participating in car pools, the poster highlights how even the smallest gestures can make a significant difference. The message of "squeeze in one more" serves as a reminder that every contribution, no matter how small, is valuable and contributes to the larger goal of winning the war. This poster, through its direct appeal and focus on community action, fosters a sense of unity and purpose among the population, encouraging individuals to see their actions as part of a collective effort to support the war.

Still More Rags for Salvage

Description: The "Still More Rags Wanted for Salvage" poster, created by John Gilroy, is a World War II propaganda piece that encourages civilians to contribute to the war effort by donating old clothing for salvage.

Significance: This poster is significant for its role in promoting the idea that every individual can contribute to the war effort, even through small actions like donating old clothing. It builds upon the theme of the "Help win the war. Squeeze in one more" poster by providing another method that civilians can use to support the soldiers. By focusing on the salvage of old clothing, the poster highlights the importance of resource conservation and the practical ways in which civilians can contribute to the war effort.

Four Freedoms Speech

Description: The document describes Franklin D. Roosevelt's (FDR) Four Freedoms speech, delivered on January 6, 1941, to Congress.

Significance: FDR's Four Freedoms speech is significant for its vision of a world founded on these four freedoms, aiming to create a new moral order distinct from the tyranny of the dictators of the time. The speech was pivotal in rallying the American people against the Axis threat and shifting public support towards assisting British and Allied troops during World War II. It marked a turning point in American foreign policy, moving the country from isolationism to active support for the Allies. The Four Freedoms speech laid the groundwork for the United Nations, emphasizing human rights and collective security.
