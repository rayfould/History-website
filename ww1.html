World War I: The Inception of Propaganda as We Know It

As we delve into the first section of our exhibition, we find ourselves at the dawn of modern propaganda. World War I marked the beginning of a new era in the use of media and psychology to influence public opinion and behavior. This section explores the early days of American propaganda, a time when the strategies were straightforward and direct, aiming to inspire and motivate the populace to support the war effort. From iconic songs like "Over There" to the distribution of savings stamps, the artifacts in this section illustrate the simplicity and directness of American propaganda during its inception.

Recruitment Artifacts

Columbia Calls - Enlist Now for U.S. Army

Description: A World War I recruitment poster created by Frances Adams Halsted in 1916, featuring a personification of America as a woman draped in the U.S. flag, urging men to enlist.

Significance: This poster is a prime example of how World War I propaganda used patriotic symbolism and allegorical figures to mobilize support for the war effort. By depicting the nation as Columbia, a strong female leader, the design appeals to ideals of masculine duty and heroism in defending one's country. The bold colors, dramatic composition, and commanding text create a sense of urgency that aimed to stir emotions and persuade men to join the fight. "Columbia Calls" illustrates the power of visual propaganda in shaping public opinion and rallying Americans behind the cause of war.

Give the World the Once Over in the United States Navy

Description: This 1919 recruitment poster shows sailors on an elephant, symbolizing the Navy's global adventures.

Significance: This poster, by James H. Daugherty, uses imagery of sailors on an elephant to represent the Navy's global missions. It appeals to recruits with the promise of adventure and exploration, suggesting that joining the Navy could offer a unique world tour. The poster reflects the Navy's strategy to attract recruits by emphasizing the exciting experiences and opportunities available in military service during WWI.

Wake Up, America!

Description: This is a 1917 poster by James Montgomery Flagg, featuring a woman in Stars & Stripes, symbolizing America asleep.

Significance: This poster, created just 13 days after the U.S. entered World War I, uses the image of a sleeping America to symbolize the nation's initial neutrality. The woman, modeled by actress Mary Arthur, represents Liberty and America, shown asleep against a fluted column and with storm clouds gathering in the background. The use of Stars & Stripes and a Phrygian cap, a symbol of freedom, emphasizes the call to action and the urgency of the moment. Flagg's artwork aimed to rouse the nation from its neutrality, highlighting the threat to civilization and the need for immediate action. This poster is a powerful example of how visual propaganda was used to mobilize support for the war effort by appealing to a sense of duty and urgency.

The Spitir of '17

Description: "The Spirit of '17" is a 1918 Paramount Pictures movie poster featuring Jack Pickford, showcasing a patriotic theme.

Significance: This poster, released shortly after the U.S. entered World War I, highlights the film's patriotic message, aiming to encourage young men to enlist. Jack Pickford, a popular actor of the time, stars in the film, which was directed by William Desmond Taylor. The poster reflects the era's focus on mobilizing support for the war effort through entertainment, using the medium of cinema to inspire and recruit.

Over There

Description: "Over There" is a song sheet featuring lyrics and music by George M. Cohan, illustrated by Norman Rockwell.

Significance: "Over There" quickly became America's national wartime ballad, capturing the nation's confidence in victory. Cohan's lyrics and Rockwell's illustration depict the camaraderie and determination of American soldiers, resonating with the public's sentiment during the war. The song sheet, with over two million copies sold, allowed families and friends to join in the song with their soldiers overseas, symbolizing unity and support for the war effort. This artifact exemplifies how popular culture, through music and art, played a crucial role in mobilizing support and fostering a sense of national unity during World War I.

War Bonds and Financing

This is your war!

Description: This poster, part of the Liberty Loan Committee's efforts, urges citizens to support the war effort by purchasing Liberty Loan bonds.

Significance: The Liberty Loan Committee's poster, created in 1918, is a key example of how the U.S. government used financial incentives to mobilize support for World War I. By subscribing to Liberty Loans, citizens could directly contribute to the war effort, with the funds used to finance the war. The poster's design, featuring a mother, father, and baby looking towards a shadow of a Prussian soldier, symbolizes the collective effort needed to defeat the enemy. This visual propaganda aimed to rally support by emphasizing the personal stake in the war and the importance of financial contributions

Charlie Chaplin and the Wall Street Factory

Description: Charlie Chaplin promoting Liberty Loans on Wall Street.

Significance: Charlie Chaplin's involvement in promoting Liberty Loans during the Third Loan Campaign at the Sub-Treasury Building on Wall Street highlights the use of celebrity endorsements to boost the war effort. Chaplin, a popular comedian and actor, used his influence to encourage Americans to buy Liberty Loan bonds. This campaign was part of a broader effort to raise funds for the war, showcasing the government's reliance on public support and the power of celebrity endorsements to mobilize resources

Remeber The Flag of Liberty

Description: A poster featuring a family with the message: "Remember! The flag of liberty! Support it. Buy us government bonds."

Significance: This poster is a significant piece of World War I propaganda, using a family to make the call to buy government bonds more relatable. It combines the American flag and a ship in the background, symbolizing unity and the path to victory. This approach highlights the importance of every citizen's financial support for the war effort, demonstrating the strategic use of imagery and text to mobilize public participation and encourage financial support for the war.

Buy War Saving Stamps

Description: An image depicting a soldier and two children, with the text: "Help him win by saving and serving! Buy war saving stamps."

Significance: This poster is a key piece of World War I propaganda, using a soldier and children to stress the importance of financial support for the war effort through buying War Savings Stamps. The soldier and children symbolize the link between the war effort and the nation's citizens, making the message about buying War Savings Stamps more personal and impactful. The inclusion of War Savings Stamps, a government-issued financial tool, shows the government's role in gathering resources for the war. This artifact shows how imagery and text were used strategically to encourage public support for the war effort, highlighting the power of propaganda in shaping public opinion and encouraging financial backing for the war.

Victory and the Spirit to Conquer

Description: An article titled "Victory and the Spirit to Conquer" with a background illustration of hundreds of people buying bonds.

Significance: This article is a significant piece of World War I propaganda, highlighting the collective effort and unity of the American people through the purchase of Liberty Bonds. The illustration of many individuals buying bonds symbolizes the nation's commitment to the war effort. It uses visual imagery to inspire and mobilize public support, emphasizing the importance of financial contributions. The title, "Victory and the Spirit to Conquer," reinforces the message of unity, determination, and the shared responsibility of achieving victory, making it a compelling piece of propaganda that effectively encourages participation in the war effort.

National Unity and Patriotism

Lullaby of War

Description: "Lullaby of War, while your daddy is far away" is a song from World War I, written by Robert S. Kampman.

Significance: This song was part of a broader effort to use music as a form of propaganda during World War I. It was sung to infants and children to explain the war and why their fathers were away. The lyrics aimed to make the war seem like a positive and noble cause, encouraging children to be brave and good while their fathers were fighting. Despite the contradictory message of a short war, these lullabies helped ease the minds of children and their worried mothers, who couldn't wait to see their soldier dads again. This use of music to influence public sentiment and support for the war effort is a clear example of how propaganda can be embedded in everyday life, affecting not just adults but also children

The Past is Behind Us

Description: A poster created by Gerrit A. Beneker in 1918.

Significance: This poster is a significant piece of World War I propaganda, designed to inspire and motivate the American public. It emphasizes the importance of looking forward to a better future, encouraging unity and optimism. The poster was part of a broader effort to maintain morale and support for the war effort during a challenging time. It reflects a sentiment of hope and progress, suggesting that the war would lead to a brighter and better future. The poster's design and message were aimed at fostering a sense of national unity and patriotism, highlighting the role of propaganda in shaping public sentiment and encouraging support for the war

True Sons of Freedom

Description: A poster depicting African-American soldiers fighting against the German army.

Significance: This poster showcases the contributions of African-American soldiers. It highlights their participation in the war, despite facing racial injustices at home. The poster symbolizes the belief that their service was a testament to their earned rights, challenging the racial injustices at home. It reflects a broader effort to acknowledge the contributions of African-Americans to the war effort and the broader struggle for equality and freedom. The inclusion of Abraham Lincoln and his quote on the poster underscores the artist's viewpoint on the contributions of African Americans to the war effort, emphasizing their role as true sons of freedom.

Together We Win

Description: A poster featuring a worker walking arm-in-arm with a sailor and a marine, symbolizing unity and cooperation.

Significance: This poster, created by James Montgomery Flagg for the United States Shipping Board Emergency Fleet Corporation, highlights the importance of unity and cooperation during World War I. It depicts a worker, a sailor, and a marine walking together, emphasizing the shared effort and support needed to achieve victory. The poster was part of a broader effort to maintain morale and support for the war effort on the home front. It reflects a sentiment of unity and cooperation, suggesting that everyone, regardless of their role, plays a crucial part in the war effort. This poster is a testament to the power of propaganda in fostering a sense of national unity and patriotism during a time of war

Music Sheet of the Star Spangled Banner

Description: The sheet music for "The Star-Spangled Banner" from 1918.

Significance: This version, orchestrated by Walter Damrosch and featuring notable figures like John Philip Sousa, was crucial for mobilizing support for the war effort. By making the song accessible and popular, it helped foster a sense of unity and patriotism among the American public. This standardization of the national anthem was part of a broader effort to use music as a tool for mobilization, reflecting the changing times and the need for a song that could resonate with the American people during wartime.

For Those Who Gave Their All

Description: "For Those Who Gave Their All" is a poem by Grantland Rice, a renowned sports writer, from World War I.

Significance: Rice's poem, known for its emotional appeal and poetic style, aimed to motivate the American public to support the war effort by highlighting the sacrifices made by soldiers. This form of propaganda was crucial in mobilizing resources and support for the war, reflecting the importance of public sentiment and unity in wartime. By focusing on the personal sacrifices and the collective effort, Rice's poem played a significant role in fostering a sense of unity and patriotism among the American public, reinforcing the idea that every contribution, no matter how small, was vital to the war effort.

Chairman of the Committee on Public Information: Complete Report

Description: A complete report of the actions of the Committee Of Public Information.

Significance: The CPI used every medium available to create enthusiasm for the war effort and enlist public support against foreign and perceived domestic attempts to stop America's participation in the war. It was a notable example of propaganda in the United States, operating for over 26 months until June 30, 1919. The committee's work was crucial in sustaining morale, releasing government news, and administering voluntary press censorship. Its activities were curtailed after the Armistice in November 1918, and it was formally disestablished by Congress on June 30, 1919, with its records turned over to the Council of National Defense

Enemy Demonization

Destroy This Mad Brute

Description: "Destroy This Mad Brute! Enlist" is a WWI poster by Harry R. Hopps, showing an angry gorilla symbolizing Germany, aiming to increase military enlistment.

Significance: This poster was instrumental in demonizing Germany and increasing American military enlistment. It was based on an earlier British version and played a significant role in shaping public perception of the enemy. The imagery of a gorilla, a symbol of brute force and savagery, was used to portray Germany as a threat to American values and civilization. This form of propaganda was effective in mobilizing support for the war, reflecting the importance of public sentiment and unity in wartime. The poster also highlighted the persecution faced by German Americans during the war, as the portrayal of Germany as a "mad brute" contributed to fear and suspicion towards German-Americans

Dont Talk!

Description: A WWI propaganda poster warning against the dangers of idle conversations, suggesting that German spies might exploit such information.

Significance: This poster was part of a broader anti-espionage campaign during WWI, aiming to raise awareness among citizens about the potential risks of revealing war secrets through casual conversation. The imagery of a spider on a web, with the head of Wilhelm II on the spider's body, symbolized the intricate and insidious nature of espionage. The caption below the title encouraged citizens to think before speaking, highlighting the importance of vigilance in protecting national security. This poster was a significant tool in the government's efforts to maintain secrecy and prevent the enemy from gaining advantage through spies.

Belgian Heroes Sacrifice

Description: The newspaper article "Belgian Heroes Sacrifice to Save Hearth and Home" highlights the bravery and resilience of Belgian soldiers and civilians during World War I, emphasizing their struggle against the German occupation.

Significance: This article aimed portray Belgium as a valiant nation united in resistance against the invader. It aimed to foster a sense of solidarity and unity among the Belgians, both in occupied territory and at the front lines. The focus on King Albert and Queen Elisabeth, along with the depiction of daily life and the aftermath of war, served to humanize the Belgian experience and garner international support. The article underscored the sacrifices made by the Belgian people to defend their homeland, using emotional imagery and narratives to evoke sympathy and support from the international community. This propaganda was crucial in maintaining morale and solidarity among the Belgians and in garnering support from the Allied nations.

Kaiser, Beast of Berlin

Description: "The Kaiser, the Beast of Berlin" is a 1918 American silent war propaganda melodrama film directed by and starring Rupert Julian. It portrays Kaiser Wilhelm II as a political beast, highlighting his greed and the resistance of his own soldiers.

Significance: This film was an effort to demonize Germany and its leadership. It aimed to influence public opinion by presenting a negative view of the German Kaiser, using him as a symbol of the war's villain. The film's portrayal of Wilhelm II as a beast was intended to undermine his authority and justify the Allied cause. Despite being lost, it remains a significant artifact of WWI propaganda, reflecting the strategic use of film to shape public perception and support for the war.

Europe's Future Map

Description: The newspaper article "Europes Future Map. Would Germany be satisfied with this?" is a WWI propaganda piece that presents a map of Europe, presumably showing the outcomes of the war.

Significance: This article was part of a broader propaganda effort to influence public opinion and support for the war. By presenting a map that depicted the future of Europe, it aimed to highlight the potential consequences of a German victory, using fear and uncertainty to encourage support for the Allied cause. The map served as a visual representation of the stakes involved in the conflict, emphasizing the importance of the war's outcome in determining the future of Europe. This form of propaganda was effective in mobilizing support and shaping public perception of the war's significance.

Conservation and Rationing

Save Food

Description: The poster is a WWI propaganda piece designed by the United States Food Administration. It encourages Americans to conserve food resources by reducing consumption of wheat, meat, fats, and sugar, thereby supporting the war effort.

Significance: This poster was part of a larger campaign by the U.S. Food Administration to mobilize the American public in the conservation of food resources during World War I. By emphasizing the need to save wheat, meat, fats, and sugar, the poster aimed to reduce food waste and ensure that these resources were available for the military and allied nations. The poster reflects the government's efforts to mobilize the food effort under a banner of volunteerism, rather than coercion, highlighting the strategic importance of conserving food resources during wartime.

Food - Dont Waste It

Description: The poster is a WWI propaganda poster that outlines six strategies for conserving food resources.

Significance: This poster was part of a broader campaign to encourage food conservation during World War I, emphasizing the importance of thoughtful purchasing, careful cooking, and reduced consumption of wheat and meat. It also promoted buying local foods and serving just enough, with the final message urging the use of what is left and not to waste it. By providing practical advice on how to reduce food waste, the poster aimed to support the war effort by ensuring that food resources were available for the military and allied nations. This form of propaganda was effective in influencing public behavior towards food conservation, contributing to the overall war effort and reflecting the strategic importance of food conservation during wartime

Food is Ammunition

Description: The poster "Food is Ammunition--don't waste it" by John E. Sheridan, created for the U.S. Food Administration in 1918.

Significance: This poster was part of a broader campaign by the U.S. Food Administration to encourage food conservation during World War I. By equating food consumption with war and using the metaphor of ammunition, the poster aimed to mobilize the American public towards conserving food resources. The strategic use of imagery and the message to consume fruits, vegetables, and fish while conserving wheat, meat, and fats for overseas shipment highlighted the government's efforts to ensure that food resources were available for the military and allied nations.

Women’s Roles in the War

American Red Cross

Description: The poster titled "You can help--American Red Cross / W. T. Benda" is a vintage piece designed by W. T. Benda, serving as a call to action for the American Red Cross during World War I.

Significance: This poster is significant as it reflects the efforts to mobilize resources and volunteerism for the war effort, with a particular emphasis on women's roles. It highlights the importance of individual contributions and the strategic use of visual communication to engage the public, especially women, who were encouraged to support the war effort through the Red Cross. The poster serves as a historical document, illustrating the methods used to foster national unity and patriotism during a critical period in American history, and it specifically addresses the unique ways in which women were involved and supported the war effort.

Your Country Needs You

Description: "Woman your country needs you!" is a color film copy transparency from 1917, featuring Liberty presenting a sword labeled "Service" to a young woman.

Significance: This poster is significant as it represents a call to women during World War I to support the war effort. It depicts Liberty, a symbol of freedom and national unity, offering a sword to a young woman, symbolizing the opportunity for women to serve their country. The poster reflects the broader societal shift during the war, encouraging women to take on roles traditionally held by men, such as working in factories and supporting the war effort. It is a historical artifact that illustrates the changing roles of women in society and their contributions to the war effort during a critical period in American history.

Significance: This poster is significant as it builds upon the themes of mobilization and the changing roles of women during World War I, echoing the sentiments expressed in the previous posters. It highlights the dual role of women as both supporters and contributors to the war effort, emphasizing their importance in both the military and industrial sectors. Significance: This article is significant as it documents a pivotal moment in women's history, marking the transition from domestic duties to active participation in the war effort. The article underscores the importance of women's contributions to the war effort, emphasizing their vital role in supporting the nation's mobilization and the military. The article also serves as a guide for women interested in assisting their country during the war time, as it provides a list of occupations women may take up while men are at war.

Women in Munition Factories

Description: The image of women working in munition factories during World War I. These women, often referred to as "munitionettes," were employed in factories to produce shells, ammunition, and other war materials, which were crucial for supporting the troops at the front.

Significance: This image is significant as it highlights the pivotal role women played in supporting the war effort, particularly in the industrial sector. The "munitionettes" played a key role in producing many important supplies for the war effort. The image also addresses the challenges and dangers faced by women workers, including working with machinery, explosives, and poisonous gases, which were very dangerous and often not adequately addressed.

Final Notes

By examining the artifacts from World War I, several key findings emerge concerning the nature of American propaganda during this period. Firstly, the simplicity and directness of the propaganda strategies are very evident. The documents and artifacts from this era reflect a clear and straightforward approach to mobilizing troops and resources. The use of iconic songs and the distribution of savings stamps were direct methods to inspire and motivate the nation, indicating a reliance on emotional appeals and the power of music to unify the nation.

As we move forward, we will witness the transformation of American propaganda from its inception, characterized by its simplicity and directness, to its evolution into a more complex and psychologically focused form. This journey through the evolution of American propaganda will provide a comprehensive understanding of how strategies and techniques have evolved, reflecting the changing nature of warfare and the role of propaganda in shaping public opinion and behavior.
